Section 1 — Executive Summary

This document is the Final Concept Design and Requirements Baseline for CVN-1, the lead ship of the Sovereign class Commonwealth Nuclear Carrier. It is presented to the Investment Approvals Board as the Main Gate submission supporting the full business case for programme approval. The document supersedes Issue 1.0 (CGB-MOD/NPSD/CVN1/2013-01) in its entirety and incorporates all decisions taken during the Assessment Phase.

CVN-1 is a new-design nuclear-powered aircraft carrier of 105,000 tonnes full-load displacement, 347 metres overall length, configured for conventional take-off and arrested landing operations with four EMALS catapults, three Advanced Arresting Gear wires, and a full-length angled deck. The propulsion plant comprises two A4W-derived pressurised water reactors delivering 280,000 shaft-horsepower across four shafts, with surplus electrical generation of 250 MW for ship's systems, future directed energy weapons, and the EMALS energy storage array. The platform is designed for a minimum fifty-year service life without hull replacement, with two planned refuelling cycles and a single mid-life modernisation.

The normal air wing of 95 aircraft — including 36 fifth-generation strike fighters, 6 AEW&C aircraft, 12 electronic attack and support aircraft, 14 maritime patrol and ASW rotary wing, and up to 18 unmanned carrier-launched aircraft at Full Operating Capability — makes CVN-1 the most capable carrier operated by any Commonwealth or allied navy outside the United States Navy. The ship is assessed as capable of independent, sustained high-intensity carrier strike operations against the most demanding threat environments in the planning horizon, including against adversaries with advanced integrated air defence and anti-ship missile systems.

Cost Summary (Outturn, Including Contingency): The confirmed whole-programme cost to IOC is estimated at £14.8 billion (2015 prices, including 15% contingency). The platform unit cost is £8.9 billion. This compares favourably with the US Navy Gerald R. Ford class at an equivalent-period unit cost of approximately £10.4 billion equivalent, attributable primarily to the use of proven A4W reactor technology, adapted UK industrial infrastructure, and a deliberate value engineering programme conducted during Assessment Phase. Full cost breakdown is provided at Section 11.

Section 2 — Strategic Requirement

2.1 Confirmed Capability Gaps

The Assessment Phase has confirmed and quantified the capability gaps identified in the Initial Procurement Dossier. Independent analysis by the Chief Scientific Adviser (Navy) and the Defence Intelligence Community has assessed the following deficiencies as critical to the Commonwealth's ability to independently conduct carrier strike operations across the planning horizon:

  • Nuclear endurance: Threat analysis confirms that adversary long-range anti-ship missile envelopes now constrain conventional carrier operational areas in two of the six priority operational theatres. Nuclear-powered sprint to favourable launch positions is the only operationally credible solution assessed.
  • Aviation capacity: Assessment of the projected air threat in priority theatres requires concurrent fleet air defence, strike, suppression of enemy air defences, and maritime patrol tasking. Modelling confirms a minimum of 90 aircraft is required for sustained independent operations; current carriers are assessed at 40 aircraft maximum.
  • Power generation: EMALS, Advanced Arresting Gear, next-generation AESA radar arrays, and the future DEW CIWS programme collectively require 180–220 MW of surplus electrical generation. This is irreconcilable with conventional propulsion at acceptable displacement.
  • Survivability: Advanced hardening, redundant damage control systems, and the ability to sustain 28+ knots under nuclear power without generating the thermal and acoustic signature of a conventionally-powered ship at maximum speed represents a survivability margin unavailable in existing conventional designs.

2.2 Strategic Policy Basis

The CVN programme is validated by the Commonwealth Defence and Security Review (2014), which assessed nuclear carrier strike as a Tier 1 national capability commitment. The Review directed that the Commonwealth must be capable of conducting sovereign carrier strike operations globally, independent of host-nation basing or partner logistics, by 2026. CVN-1 is the sole programme assessed as meeting this requirement within the declared budget envelope. The programme has received cross-party parliamentary endorsement and is designated a protected programme within HM Treasury's capital investment framework.

Section 3 — Platform Overview

CVN-1 is designated the lead ship of the Sovereign class. The class name reflects the strategic significance of the platform as an instrument of independent Commonwealth power projection. The ship will be laid down at a consortium of three shipbuilding yards under a national industrial partnership arrangement, with final assembly at the designated assembly facility on the Clyde. The Sovereign class design is a new-design original Commonwealth hull, drawing on technical data exchange under the AUKUS and Five Power Defence Arrangement frameworks.

The design employs a twin-island configuration, separating navigation and air traffic control functions (forward island) from aviation command and combat direction functions (aft island). This arrangement, validated by assessment of the USN Gerald R. Ford class, reduces electromagnetic interference between the two island functions, improves sightlines for both bridge watch and aviation direction teams, and reduces the ship's radar cross-section contribution from superstructure compared to a single large island. The twin islands are connected by an enclosed weather-deck walkway at sponson level.

5.1 Reactor Plant — Confirmed Selection

Following Assessment Phase evaluation of three candidate reactor plant configurations, the A4W pressurised water reactor — as operated by the USN in the Nimitz class — has been selected as the basis for the CVN-1 propulsion plant, subject to a Commonwealth-specific design adaptation programme under joint oversight of NRSA and the designated reactor system designer. Selection was made on the basis of proven operational reliability, the availability of a substantial operational experience base across the Nimitz class fleet, the existence of a mature fuel and maintenance supply chain accessible under AUKUS Technical and Industrial Cooperation arrangements, and the lowest whole-life cost of the three candidates assessed.

5.2 Propulsion Resilience & Nuclear Safety Philosophy

The plant is designed for single-reactor operation at reduced speed in the event of a casualty to either reactor or associated machinery train. Single-reactor operation sustains 22 knots, sufficient for independent transit and continuation of flying operations in all but the most demanding threat environments. Emergency propulsion capability is provided by emergency propulsion motors (EPM) on two shafts, capable of sustaining 8 knots for harbour transit under total main machinery loss.

  • Complete physical and system isolation between the two reactor plants; no shared primary circuit components
  • Emergency core cooling and passive shutdown systems compliant with NRSA Generation III+ standards
  • Reactor compartment designed to contain a maximum credible accident without release of radioactive contamination beyond the compartment boundary in any sea state to Beaufort 12
  • Ship's nuclear emergency organisation compliant with NPSD/NRSA/003; dedicated nuclear damage control parties
  • Continuous nuclear safety surveillance by NRSA inspector embarked during operational deployments exceeding 30 days

Section 7 — Combat Systems & Self-Defence

The Sovereign class self-defence philosophy is layered, deeply redundant, and makes explicit provision for directed energy close-in defence systems in the growth baseline. The design moves beyond the minimal self-defence approach of the Issue 1.0 dossier following threat assessment updates which identified a materially increased saturation anti-ship missile threat from two peer-competitor states in the priority operational theatres. The ship is not a surface combatant, but must be capable of independent survival against a coordinated multi-axis saturation attack while the air wing is launched or recovered.

Section 9 — Unmanned Systems Integration

9.1 Unmanned Carrier-Launched Aircraft (UCLA)

The Sovereign class is designed from keel to mast as an unmanned-capable carrier. The Assessment Phase confirmed that the EMALS / AAG combination, deck-edge elevator capacity, and hangar infrastructure are fully compatible with current and all projected UCLA types across the 50-year hull life. The UAS integration architecture is defined in the ship's combat management system as a native module, not a retrofit, reflecting the decision taken at Issue 1.3 to treat unmanned operations as a first-class mission rather than a growth provision.

IOC UCLA Capability6 × ISR/light-strike UCLA; supervised autonomous operation; all weapons releases human-in-the-loop at IOC
FOC UCLA Capability18 × UCLA (mixed ISR, strike, and tanker roles); includes provision for attritable lower-cost types alongside high-end persistent ISR/strike platforms
Attritable UAS ProvisionNEW AT ISS 2.0 2 × 20-round tube-launch attritable UAS systems in bow sponsons; provides organic counter-drone swarm capability and expendable ISR at short notice without EMALS commitment

Section 11 — Whole-Life Cost Estimate & Value Engineering [NEW]

The following cost estimates have been developed by DE&S in conjunction with the Industrial Partnering Team and reviewed by HM Treasury (Major Projects) and the Infrastructure and Projects Authority. All figures are in 2015 outturn prices unless stated. The programme has been subject to a dedicated Value Engineering workstream throughout Assessment Phase, with the objective of delivering the required capability at the minimum credible cost consistent with programme schedule and nuclear safety requirements. The headline conclusion is that the Sovereign class can be delivered at a unit cost approximately 15% below the Gerald R. Ford-class equivalent, primarily through strategic technology choices rather than capability reduction.

11.2 Value Engineering — Key Decisions and Savings

The Assessment Phase Value Engineering programme identified £2.1 billion in savings against the reference design cost without capability reduction. The principal decisions are summarised below:

VE DecisionSavingRationale

CAMM-ER over ESSM / Standard for VLS~£190MCommonwealth sovereign missile eliminates US FMS dependency; lower unit cost; quad-pack compatibility allows same cell count to deliver greater magazine depth

Cost Comparison Context: The confirmed £9.9 billion whole-programme cost to IOC compares to: USS Gerald R. Ford (CVN-78): approximately £12.8 billion equivalent (2015 prices); HMS Queen Elizabeth (R08, conventional): £3.1 billion (non-nuclear, ski-jump, no EMALS, smaller air wing). The programme represents a credible and competitive cost for the capability delivered. Programme Risk Summary (IPA Assessment — March 2015): Overall IPA rating: AMBER-RED.
